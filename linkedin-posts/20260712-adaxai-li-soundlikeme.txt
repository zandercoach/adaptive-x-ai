Adaptive × AI — teaching the AI to sound like me

Next experiment on my learning journey on adaptive-x-ai: LinkedIn posts from the lab. The idea: my AI agent drafts, I check and post. So the agent suggested analyzing my writing style first, to make the drafts sound like me. Good idea, I thought.

It came back with a confident style profile, based on the three most recent posts in this series. Small problem: those posts were mostly written by the agent itself, lightly edited by me. It was reading its own style back to itself and calling it mine.

So we started over, this time with ground truth: five posts I had actually written myself, from workshop recaps to the story of my kids joining our family's weekly planning. Plus an interview, one question at a time: language, tone, endings, taboos. The result is a versioned style guide in the repo that every future draft gets checked against.

My favorite finding: the em-dash. The agent's most-used punctuation mark, and one of the clearest "an AI wrote this" signals. It is now rationed — at most two per post. (That was one.)

The general lesson: an AI fed with its own output converges on itself, not on you. If it should represent you, give it evidence of you — and check that the evidence isn't circular.

Full disclosure, as always: this is not a "here's how you do it" post. It's a learning report: what I tried, what broke, what I got wrong. The full journal, including the mistakes, is public (link in the first comment).

Next up: the first posts drafted from the new repertoire, in German for a change. More to come, warts included.
