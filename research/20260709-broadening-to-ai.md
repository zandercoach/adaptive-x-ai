## Technical Learnings

- A GitHub repo rename is low-risk: permanent redirects cover both web URLs and git remotes, so published links survive, and `gh repo rename` updates the local remote in the same step.
- Renaming the local folder is the one step that can't happen mid-session: the running Claude Code session holds the directory, and its project-scoped state (settings, memory, session history under `~/.claude/projects/`) is keyed to the path — so the folder rename and the matching state-folder rename belong after the session ends.
- Second reframe in under a month, and the 2026-06-22 lesson held: framing churn is cheap when caught early. The cost was one README pass plus two one-line updates — not a rewrite of history, because journal entries are immutable records of their moment.
- The broadening required no structural change: the three lenses (business, leadership, operating model) already generalized to AI — only the disruption's name and the scope of the questions were narrow. A hint that the 2026-06-22 restructuring got the load-bearing part right.

## Organizational Learnings

- The learning is three-fold. First, you learn as fast as you go: I can try, evaluate, and build faster than ever, because the actual doing can be well-assisted via AI.
- Second, pivoting becomes easier as well: putting ideas into practice, changing things you've already done — all that can be well-assisted, too.
- Third, from learning how to use agentic engineering, there's just a small step and some kind of natural way to broaden my perspective on the impact AI will have. I just wanted to learn AI-assisted, agentic coding, but my mind jumped faster and further with every new learning: If building is easy and fast, why not evaluate more ideas? If I can evaluate more ideas in the same time, what should an organization look like that is capable of adapting to this rate of innovation and change? What other kind of impact will AI have on the business model — what are the shifts in business, technology, and leadership? How can and will AI support this adaptiveness, and what impact will this support have on our way of organizing work? Questions I asked myself in the beginning now not only influence, but direct my endeavor of learning the impact of AI.

## Leadership Perspective

- When I use an AI agent as a sparring partner, it's crucial to provide the right context, with the data needed to work on a specific goal, not just a task. So goal-setting, context, and boundaries are necessary — boundaries also to keep AI within a given spec. Clarity in communication is crucial, otherwise there will be a form of "shit in — shit out".
- It's necessary to be in the loop and in contact with agents. Stay in charge: provide clear context and goal, assess intended or proposed ways to work on that goal, review results, and provide clear, constructive feedback. This way, AI agents can learn from feedback and communication to become even better over time.
- When looking at an adaptive organization, working with the people follows the same guidelines. Being adaptive also means handling change in an effective, constructive, and business-supporting way. Change is faster, and so needs the organization to be. To be fast means to decide fast in contexts of uncertainty (and all the other aspects of VUCA), to inspect the impact of those decisions continuously, and to adapt based on the feedback, market changes, and so on.
